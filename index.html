Personal Log - Entry 001

[TIMESTAMP: 2846.11.23 - 02:17:45]
[CLASSIFICATION: PERSONAL]
[STATUS: ARCHIVED]

It started with mandatory reading in French lyc√©e. Holocaust literature. While my classmates fed Primo Levi to their AIs for summaries, I couldn't stop reading. Man's Search for Meaning. The Pianist. The Gulag Archipelago. Something about the systematic nature of it all. The bureaucracy. The forms filled out in triplicate for mass murder.

But it was Browning's Ordinary Men that provided the key insight. Reserve Police Battalion 101. Regular middle-aged Germans. Family men. Turned into executioners in occupied Poland.

The most terrifying part wasn't the ideology. It was the logistics.

Browning describes how the battalion struggled with their rifles at first. High-powered 7.62mm cartridges. When aimed at the head, they would... explode. Brain matter. Bone fragments. Blood spray coating uniforms.

So they adapted. Learned to aim for the neck instead. Cleaner. More efficient. Less laundry.

This wasn't ideology driving improvement. This was ordinary human problem-solving. Men who wanted to go home with clean uniforms finding better ways to murder children.

They weren't Nazis. They were just people who got good at their job.

Research Methodology

[TIMESTAMP: 2846.12.15 - 14:33:12]
[CLASSIFICATION: RESEARCH NOTES]
[STATUS: ONGOING]

The question consumed me: Why?

I enrolled in economics at university, convinced that economic forces shaped modern society more than anything else. If I wanted to understand how we got here, I needed to understand the mechanisms of power and resource distribution.

But capitalism was too recent. Too narrow. I kept studying history on my own, working backwards through time. Industrial Revolution. Age of Sail. Renaissance. All the way to antiquity. Economic patterns, yes, but also military structures, social hierarchies, religious systems.

Late nights cross-referencing data. Atrocities across millennia. I wasn't trained as a historian. It was just compulsion driving research.

With both lenses together, economic analysis and historical patterns, that's where it emerged.
The pattern.

We aren't evil by nature. But we are survivors. And survival requires rationalization. We can justify almost anything if it means living another day, keeping our families fed, maintaining our position in the hierarchy.

Every civilization reflects this capacity. Every golden age built on someone else's bones. Every moral code with convenient exceptions for the necessary evils.

Experimental Parameters

[TIMESTAMP: 2847.01.08 - 23:45:01]
[CLASSIFICATION: SIMULATION DATA]
[STATUS: ACTIVE MONITORING]

So I ran simulations.

My second education in game design and production provided the methodological framework. Understanding player psychology, decision trees, and behavioral incentives under pressure proved directly applicable to civilizational analysis. Games, after all, are systems designed to reveal authentic choice patterns when subjects believe the stakes matter.

Given certain parameters in specific environments, how would we act? I had millennia of human history as my control group. Nudge an independent variable here. Alter social pressures there. Analyze the outcomes.

The results were always the same.

Different settings. Different technologies. Different ideologies. But the core pattern never changed. Rationalization. Adaptation. Whatever it took to preserve survival and social position. Sometimes it got extreme. Sometimes entire populations disappeared.

But the systems achieved stability. At least for a while.

These testimonies document my experimental parameters. Each story represents a different configuration: variables adjusted to test the limits of human moral flexibility under pressure.

The subjects never knew they were being observed.

Personal Log - Entry 004

[TIMESTAMP: 2847.02.28 - 01:12:33]
[CLASSIFICATION: PERSONAL]
[STATUS: FINAL ENTRY]

The cosmic horror of it isn't the supernatural elements. It's recognizing ourselves in every protagonist.

In Nazi Germany, would you have been a Nazi if your children were starving during the Great Depression?

In ancient Rome, would you have owned slaves if you could afford them? How far would you have "used" them?

In an agrarian society, would you have supported patriarchy if male-line inheritance was the most efficient way to secure your territory and expand it? How long before you started believing women were naturally inferior to justify the system you needed?

And what if the parameters were different? What if magic existed and required human sacrifice for power? What if we had star-powered AI and relativistic kill vehicles?

These simulations answer those questions.

I've arranged them by accessibility, not chronology. Start with the shorts if you can stomach them. The longer works require commitment.

You might think you know how you'd act. But real understanding only comes from living through the choice.

Once you see the pattern, you'll recognize it everywhere.
